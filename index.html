Calculating Pi

π is the ratio between the circumference and the diameter of a circle on a flat plane.

`pi = "circumference" / "diameter" `

The ratio is the same for any size circle. Try measuring π yourself on plates and bowls and bicycle wheels!

The value of π is about 3.14 which is about 22 ÷ 7. But π is an irrational number. It can't be written accurately as a fraction of whole numbers. The digits of π can never be written down completely - it would require an infinite number of digits. Writing all those digits would take forever, and besides, no one knows what all the digits are!

Even though mathematicians have known about π for thousands of years, new ways to calculate π are still being discovered. Below are a few famous ways mathematicians have discovered to calculate more and more of π.

Estimate π with an infinite series discovered in the 15th century.

The Indian mathematician Nilakantha Somayaji reported this infinite series. It converges to π:

`π = 3 + 4 / ((3^3 - 3)) - 4 / ((5^3 - 5)) + 4 / ((7^3 - 7)) - 4 / ((9^3-9)) + ... `

which is equivalent to the following (can you see why?):

`π = 3 + 4 / ((2 * 3 * 4)) - 4 / ((4 * 5 * 6)) + 4 / ((6 * 7 * 8)) - 4 / ((8 * 9 * 10)) + ... `

Each term in the series is smaller than the last. As we add more and more terms together, the sum gets closer and closer to π.
